AI Foundations

Courseware Archive • Module 1

Module 1 introduces the foundations of Artificial Intelligence, focusing on the ideas that make modern AI different from traditional computing.

1.1 Beyond the Hype: Defining Artificial Intelligence

At its core, Artificial Intelligence is not a single technology but a field of computer science focused on creating systems that mimic cognitive functions—specifically learning, reasoning, and problem-solving.

While classical computing relies on rigid, rule-based systems (if-then-else logic), AI shifts the paradigm toward probabilistic systems. We move from telling a computer how to solve a problem to providing it with the environment to learn how to solve it.

1.2 The Paradigm Shift: From Rules to Data

The most critical distinction in modern AI is the transition from Symbolic AI (Good Old-Fashioned AI) to Connectionism (Machine Learning).

  • Symbolic AI: Relies on human-curated knowledge bases and explicit logic.
  • Machine Learning (ML): Utilizes algorithms to identify statistical patterns within large datasets.

1.3 The Core Components

To build an intelligent agent, three pillars are required:

Data: The empirical evidence used for training. Algorithms: The mathematical models (e.g., linear regression, neural networks) that extract patterns. Compute: The hardware capacity (GPUs/TPUs) required to iterate through complex calculations efficiently.

1.4 Discussion Prompt Seminar Prompt

We often refer to AI as "intelligent," yet it operates on statistical inference rather than conscious understanding.

"Does the performance of a model accurately represent 'intelligence,' or is it simply a sophisticated form of pattern recognition?"
